Did you Know?


  • Zenodo is derived from Zenodotus, the first librarian of the Ancient Library of Alexandria and father of the first recorded use of metadata, a landmark in library history.

Some Specifications


  • Original author: OpenAIRE and CERN
  • Original release: 2013
  • Stable release: 1.10 (August 2015)
  • Supported Operating Systems: Windows, macOS, Linux

What is so cool about this tool?

Zenodo is a general purpose open-access, research data repository that allows anyone and everyone access and the ability to upload research. It has been a great resource for data archiving and sharing in libraries and for understanding how to properly cite research data/datasets, just to name two of the many features it has to offer!

Much like Academia.edu, which is a large (30 million members strong) social media networking site for academics/researchers to come on, create profiles, and share any of their research, published or unpublished, Zenodo allows a researcher to do the same with papers, and more technical things that include code and datasets. This repository is certainly innovative because it emphasizes global collaboration, having researchers of similar academic communities—or even different—share their content/research for anyone and everyone. The primary goal of Zenodo is much like Academia.edu and it was built and developed for researchers, as they say to make sure everyone can join in Open Science! Zenodo creators said that the digital revolution is in need of a retooling on how scholarly processes and libraries manage and handle data and software, and the first natural step is to make scholarly data and software open-access to everyone.

Is this tool being used extensively?

Not entirely. Researchers in Zenodo have uploaded by now tens of thousands of research papers/data and datasets on said repository. Because of the uploads, there have been thousands of different communities such as the Human Brain Project and the Digital Historical Linguistics community that publishes works specific to their field.


Benefits of Open-Source Tools

  • 1. Free and typically secure!
  • 2. Anyone can fix bugs that are found without waiting for next release.
  • 3. Not dependent on the original author of company that created it. The code can be modified however the end-user sees fit

Negatives of Open Source Technology

  • 1. Not very straightforward to use. May require training.
  • 2. Many of the latest hardward is incompatible with the open-source platform meaning reliance on third-party drivers.
  • 3. User interfaces are not too attractive.

Advantages of Zenodo

  • 1. Your data will be given a unique digital identifier (DOI) making it more accessible and citable!
  • 2. Zenodo includes options to allow you to select access rights, access conditions, and licenses for your uploads.
  • 3. Your data will be preserved! CERN (a large data center with petabytes of data) developed and manages Zenodo. Your data will live on CERN servers in the CERN Data Center.

Disadvantages of Zenodo

  • 1. Once a file is published, you are not able to make changes to the file on record.
  • 2. The Zenodo community is still relatively small with tens of thousands of users
  • 3. Does not have an advanced search syntax for ease in searching on all metadata available in Zenodo
